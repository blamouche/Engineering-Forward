# Pour OpenAI, 2026 est l'année de tous les dangers

**Source**: https://www.journaldunet.com/intelligence-artificielle/1547047-pour-openai-2026-est-l-annee-de-tous-les-dangers/

**Date**: January 7, 2026

**Author**: Guillaume Renouard

**Keywords**: OpenAI, AI funding, artificial intelligence, tech industry, startup economics, AI competition, computing infrastructure

## Elevator pitch

OpenAI faces an existential year in 2026, burning through $17 billion in cash while pursuing an unprecedented $100 billion fundraising round amid mounting competitive pressure from Google and Chinese open-source models.

## Takeaways

- OpenAI will burn $17 billion in cash in 2026, nearly double the $9 billion spent in 2024, with inference costs exceeding revenue during H1 2025
- The company is pursuing a $100 billion fundraising round after securing $40 billion in March 2025, but profitability isn't expected until 2030
- Computing power needs have exploded from 200 megawatts in 2023 to 1.9 gigawatts in 2025, with plans to add 30 more gigawatts
- Google's Gemini advances and Chinese open-source models threaten OpenAI's market dominance and complicate future fundraising narratives
- Strategic options include vertical integration through custom chips via Broadcom, enterprise expansion, and consumer hardware collaboration with Jony Ive

## Synthesis

OpenAI enters 2026 at a critical inflection point that will likely determine the company's long-term viability. The organization that popularized generative AI with ChatGPT now faces a confluence of financial, competitive, and operational pressures that dwarf anything previously seen in the technology sector.

The financial picture is stark. OpenAI plans to burn through approximately $17 billion in cash this year, representing a near-doubling from the $9 billion consumed in 2024. This astronomical burn rate stems from the fundamental economics of operating large language models at scale. Expert Gary Marcus noted that during the first half of 2025, inference costs actually exceeded revenue—a troubling dynamic for any business but particularly alarming at OpenAI's scale.

To address this capital intensity, OpenAI is pursuing what would be the largest private fundraising round in history: $100 billion. This follows the $40 billion secured in March 2025. According to HSBC analysis, the company won't achieve profitability until 2030, meaning investors are being asked to finance at least four more years of significant losses before seeing returns.

The infrastructure demands underscore why costs have spiraled. OpenAI's computing power requirements have grown nearly tenfold, from 200 megawatts in 2023 to 1.9 gigawatts in 2025. The company has announced plans to add another 30 gigawatts of capacity—power consumption rivaling small nations. This creates not just financial strain but also operational and environmental challenges.

Competitive dynamics add another layer of complexity. Google's Gemini has matured into a serious competitor, while Chinese open-source models have democratized access to capable AI systems. These developments undermine the narrative that justified OpenAI's premium valuations: that it possessed an unassailable technological lead. As competitors catch up, the case for continued mega-investments becomes harder to articulate.

Management is exploring several strategic pivots. Vertical integration through custom chip development with Broadcom could reduce dependency on Nvidia and potentially lower inference costs. Enterprise expansion offers more predictable revenue streams. Perhaps most intriguingly, a collaboration with former Apple designer Jony Ive on consumer hardware suggests OpenAI may attempt to create new product categories entirely.

Historical parallels offer both hope and caution. Amazon and Netflix both sustained years of losses before achieving dominance in their respective markets. However, OpenAI's scale of losses is unprecedented, and the AI market's eventual structure remains unclear. The company may achieve consolidation to emerge as a dominant player, or it might receive implicit "too big to fail" support given the strategic importance of AI leadership. Yet bankruptcy remains possible if strategic breakthroughs don't materialize and investor patience wanes. For OpenAI, 2026 truly represents the year of all dangers.

# Why Senior Engineers Let Bad Projects Fail

**Source**: https://lalitm.com/post/why-senior-engineers-let-bad-projects-fail/

**Date**: January 13, 2026

**Author**: Lalit Maganti

**Keywords**: senior engineering, organizational influence, technical leadership, career strategy, project management, engineering politics

## Elevator pitch

Senior engineers must learn that being correct matters less than being effective—strategic restraint in opposing bad projects preserves the influence needed to win battles that actually matter.

## Takeaways

- Influence operates like a bank account: minor objections cost little, architectural pushbacks cost more, and attempting to kill executive initiatives can bankrupt your credibility entirely
- Bad projects fall into three categories: user experience flaws (overcomplication, solving non-problems), technical issues (poor architecture, wrong tooling), and political motivations (trend-chasing, promotion-driven decisions)
- When deciding whether to intervene, evaluate proximity to your team, impact on your work if the project fails, and company-scale consequences of failure
- Intervention strategies range from direct escalation to subtle contingency planning to complete non-involvement—choosing the right approach depends on the specific situation
- Honest communication with your own team about risks is essential, but sharing unnecessary political details creates problems without benefits

## Synthesis

Lalit Maganti's essay addresses a reality that technical leadership literature often ignores: experienced engineers frequently observe projects headed for failure yet choose not to intervene. Rather than framing this as cynicism or abdication, Maganti presents it as strategic competence—the mature recognition that being correct and being effective are different things.

The financial metaphor for influence is the article's central framework. Every intervention withdraws from a limited credibility account. Raising small objections in meetings costs little. Pushing back on architectural decisions costs substantially more. Attempting to shut down executive-sponsored initiatives can bankrupt years of accumulated trust in a single transaction. Engineers who spend freely on minor complaints find themselves unable to afford intervention when it genuinely matters.

The taxonomy of bad projects is useful for calibration. Some projects fail because they misunderstand users—overcomplicating simple problems or building solutions for non-existent needs. Others fail for technical reasons: poorly chosen architectures, inappropriate tooling, or fundamental design flaws. The politically motivated category may be hardest to address: projects that exist to justify promotions, satisfy trend-following executives, or create empire-building opportunities. Each category presents different risks and different intervention possibilities.

Maganti's decision framework operationalizes when to spend influence. Proximity determines how much a failure affects your daily work. Impact measures whether failure will cascade into your responsibilities. Company-scale consequences determine whether the failure threatens organizational viability or merely wastes resources in an isolated corner. Projects that score high on all three dimensions justify significant influence expenditure. Those scoring low may warrant nothing more than documented concerns filed away for future reference.

The intervention spectrum acknowledges that opposition isn't binary. Direct escalation represents the nuclear option—effective when successful but expensive in credibility regardless of outcome. Subtle contingency planning positions your team to survive or recover from anticipated failure without overt opposition. Complete non-involvement preserves influence entirely while accepting that some failures must run their course.

The emphasis on honest team communication deserves attention. Maganti distinguishes between appropriate transparency—helping your team understand risks and prepare accordingly—and inappropriate detail-sharing that creates political complications without enabling useful action. Teams need enough information to make good decisions; they don't need to absorb their leader's frustrations about organizational dysfunction.

The essay implicitly argues that sustainable technical leadership requires accepting uncomfortable trade-offs. Fighting every battle guarantees losing the ones that matter. Strategic restraint isn't surrender; it's resource allocation for an extended campaign rather than a single engagement.

# Beyond Senior: Consider the staff path!

**Source**: https://hawksley.org/2026/01/14/beyond-senior.html

**Date**: January 14, 2026

**Author**: Joel Hawksley

**Keywords**: staff engineer, career progression, technical leadership, engineering management, individual contributor, archetypes

## Elevator pitch

The staff engineer role offers senior engineers an alternative to management for career advancement, operating at broader scope across teams through four distinct archetypes while delivering impact through strategic technical work and organizational influence.

## Takeaways

- Staff engineers operate at broader scope than seniors—either working across multiple teams or diving deeper into specialized technical areas—without transitioning to people management
- Four staff archetypes define different ways to operate: Tech Lead (guiding team execution), Architect (setting technical direction), Solver (tackling complex problems independently), and Right Hand (extending executive attention)
- Maintaining a public work journal helps identify impactful contributions, communicates role value organizationally, and protects against imposter syndrome through visible accomplishment records
- Disambiguation before delegation—using prototypes and research to reduce ambiguity—enables effective handoff of clarified problems to senior engineers and teams
- Staff engineers generate organizational energy through mentorship, pairing, and actively elevating colleagues toward promotion rather than hoarding knowledge or opportunities

## Synthesis

Joel Hawksley's talk, presented at Boulder Ruby, provides a practitioner's guide to the staff engineer role for senior engineers considering their next career step. Rather than treating staff engineering as an abstract concept, Hawksley draws on his own progression from news photographer through junior apprentice to staff engineer at GitHub, offering concrete guidance on what the role involves and how to succeed in it.

The fundamental distinction between senior and staff engineers is scope. Senior engineers excel within their team's domain. Staff engineers operate across team boundaries or dive significantly deeper into specialized technical areas. Hawksley uses a rope analogy to illustrate progression depth: juniors learn to hold the rope, mid-levels climb proficiently, seniors teach others to climb, and staff engineers may be establishing new routes entirely or training other climbing instructors.

The four archetypes framework helps clarify what staff roles actually look like in practice. Tech Leads partner with engineering managers to guide team execution and technical approach. Architects own direction and quality for critical technical areas spanning multiple teams. Solvers parachute into arbitrarily complex problems and work independently until resolution. Right Hands extend executive attention, acting as leadership force multipliers for CTOs or VPs who cannot personally engage everywhere.

Hawksley's practical strategies reflect hard-won lessons about succeeding at staff level. Regular archetype alignment conversations with managers prevent the dangerous drift where expectations diverge—a particular risk since staff roles are often poorly defined and highly varied. The public work journal practice addresses both external and internal needs: it demonstrates value to the organization while also providing the engineer with concrete evidence of accomplishment during periods when impact feels uncertain.

The "predict the future" strategy positions staff engineers as organizational sensors, staying informed about both internal roadmaps and external industry trends. This knowledge makes them valuable consultants to teams and leaders navigating uncertainty. "Speak truth to power" emphasizes using data to surface engineering priorities, creating shared understanding between product and technical disciplines without becoming confrontational.

The disambiguation principle is particularly valuable. Many problems that appear to require staff-level attention are actually well-defined problems obscured by ambiguity. Staff engineers create leverage by investing in prototypes, research, and specifications that clarify what needs to be built, then delegating the now-tractable implementation to senior engineers. This multiplies impact beyond what individual contribution could achieve.

Hawksley closes by emphasizing that generating energy—through mentorship, pairing, and actively developing others toward promotion—distinguishes great staff engineers from merely competent ones. The role isn't just about personal technical accomplishment but about raising the capabilities of everyone around you.
